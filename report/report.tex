\section{Motivation and question}

Standard reinforcement learning knows the dynamics $T$ of a Markov decision process and wants the optimal policy $\pi^{*}$. Many real settings invert that assumption: \cite{benac2024itl} uses an intensive care unit as its running example, where outcomes are observable but how a patient responds to a particular dose is not, and what we see is a batch of clinician decisions that are close to optimal but not exactly optimal.

My broader interest sits downstream of this in personalized wellness technology: the wearable stack (Apple Watch, Oura, WHOOP, continuous glucose monitors). Most consumer pipelines fit a supervised model from user action to outcome, treating every body as a shared-dynamics black box and prescribing one-size-fits-all interventions. Inverse Transition Learning (ITL) takes a different posture: the user is a near-optimal decision-maker whose actions encode information about their own physiology, and that signal is exactly what maximum-likelihood-over-counts throws away. The semester's technical question: given $R$ and a batch of $\varepsilon$-optimal expert transitions $D = \{(s, a, s')\}$, can we recover $T^{*}$ in a way that respects the optimality information in the actions?

\section{Method and tools}

An action $a$ is $\varepsilon$-optimal at state $s$ when $\max_{a'} Q^{*}(s, a'; T) - Q^{*}(s, a; T) \le \varepsilon$. Observing the expert choose $a$ at $s$ yields an inequality on $Q^{*}$. Writing the policy-evaluation inverse $v^{\pi} = (I - \gamma P^{\pi})^{-1} r^{\pi}$ and substituting the Laplace-smoothed MLE $T^{\mathrm{MLE}}$ inside it linearizes the constraint in $T$, giving
\begin{equation*}
\min_{T} \;\; \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{s,a,s'} N_{s,a,s'} \bigl(T_{s,a,s'} - T^{\mathrm{MLE}}_{s,a,s'}\bigr)^{2}
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad
\text{$\varepsilon$-ball on $(s,a) \in D$, simplex on $T$.}
\end{equation*}
The solver alternates between $\hat\pi$ (uniform over observed actions; uniform over all actions at unobserved states) and the QP, terminating when the $\varepsilon$-ball property holds at every observed state (Fig.~\ref{fig:mech}a). The convex pieces map onto Krause and H\"ubotter \cite[Ch.~10--11]{krause2025pai}: Bellman optimality, the linear policy-evaluation system, and the Dirichlet--Categorical conjugacy that makes the Laplace-smoothed MLE a posterior mean rather than a frequentist estimate. Implementation: CVXPY \cite{diamond2016cvxpy} with an OSQP--SCS--CLARABEL fallback chain.

\section{Simulation: coverage sweep on Gridworld}

I reproduced the paper's central claim (their Figure~2) on a 5$\times$5 Gridworld with soft walls (penalty $-5$), slip $0.2$, $\gamma{=}0.95$, $\varepsilon{=}5.0$, 40\%-stochastic expert. \emph{Coverage} is the fraction of states appearing in $D$; for each observed $(s,a)$ I sampled 10 transitions. At full coverage, ITL achieves Normalized Value $0.998 \pm 0.001$ vs.\ MLE's $0.142 \pm 0.002$, with $0$ vs.\ $7.20$ constraint violations and $\varepsilon$-matching $1.000$ vs.\ $0.712$ (5 seeds; Fig.~\ref{fig:mech}c). MLE under-counts visited next-state cells under a stochastic expert, so its greedy policy walks into soft walls; ITL forces the expert's observed choices to remain $\varepsilon$-optimal under $\hat T$. Shape matches the paper's Figure~2; absolute scale runs $\sim 1.5\times$ larger than Table~4, almost certainly because the soft-wall layout is not published in detail. The structural pattern (ITL $\ge$ MLE on every metric, $\varepsilon$-matching $\to 1.0$ at full coverage per Theorem~1) holds on both Gridworld and RandomWorld.

\section{Open question: joint reward and dynamics recovery}

The paper assumes $R$ is known. In MIMIC-IV that is fragile: the survival proxy reward ($\pm 15$ for discharge or mortality, after \cite{komorowski2018aic}) is hand-engineered. The extension I have been prototyping, named in the paper's future-work section, lifts $R$ into a decision variable $R(s,a) = \Phi(s,a)^{\top} w$ and solves for $(T, w)$ jointly (Fig.~\ref{fig:mech}b). The $\varepsilon$-ball constraint becomes
\begin{equation*}
\bigl(\Phi(s,a) - \Phi(s,a')\bigr)^{\top} w + \gamma\,(T_{s,a} - T_{s,a'})^{\top} v_{\mathrm{lin}} \;\ge\; \varepsilon,
\end{equation*}
bilinear in $(T, w)$ but a convex QP at each step of the alternation (hold $w$, solve for $T$; hold $T$, solve for $w$). An L1 penalty on $w$ plus an anchor at one $(s,a)$ pin the additive ambiguity. The prototype converges to a fixed point in two outer iterations on both the 3-state corridor and the 5$\times$5 Gridworld. On Gridworld with one-hot $(s,a)$ features ($d=100$), goal-anchored at $R(s^{*},0)=10$, $\lambda_{L1}=0.01$, deterministic expert: ITL+IRL recovers the optimal policy exactly (best-action $1.000$ vs.\ ITL with known $R$ $1.000$ vs.\ MLE $0.600$); $T$ MSE $0.4873$ vs.\ $0.4929$ (ITL) vs.\ $0.5154$ (MLE). \emph{Honest caveat:} this gridworld is goal-dominated --- pairing $T_{\mathrm{MLE}}$ with a trivial reward $R{=}10\cdot\mathbf{1}[s{=}s^{*}]$ recovers $\pi^{*}$ at the same rate as pairing it with $R^{*}$, so a $1.000$ best-action match is necessary but not sufficient evidence that the IRL step is doing real work. A two-goal benchmark ($R_A{=}5$, $R_B{=}10$) and a head-to-head against MaxEnt-IRL \cite{ziebart2008maxent} (wired in via L-BFGS-B with a null-space anchor) are the next falsifiers before May~31.

\paragraph{Implications for personalized wellness.} The reproduction confirms that under near-optimal behavior, individual-level dynamics are recoverable from sparse demonstrations in a way supervised models miss. The joint reward extension matters more for wearables than the ICU: sleep-quality and recovery scores are noisy user proxies, so jointly inferring $R$ with $T$ removes the most fragile assumption in any wearables pipeline. The continuous-state lift is the gating step before this transfers to raw sensor streams.
